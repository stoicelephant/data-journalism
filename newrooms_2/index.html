Dead Internet Theory: The killing of creativity, perception and certainty

By: Carly Wolfe, James Giuffre, and Fayaz Qadeer

Have you ever entered a comment section and were greeted with comments that almost seem to repeat themselves? If you have been on the internet within the past decade, the answer is most likely yes. Next time you notice this, look at the profile photo of the commenters. What do you notice? If you notice that the profile photo attached to this comment is either a) lacking a photo of a person, or b) containing an image of a person who seems to be created by artificial intelligence, then you have been introduced to the Dead Internet Theory.

This idea centers around the belief that the spread of bot activity and automated responses is starting to overtake human activity on the internet and social media platforms. Since there is a possibility that a lot of people you interact with online are not real, and the possibility has increased within the last couple of years due to AI, we are seeing this “theory” assimilate into our world upon our own very eyes.

To understand why this issue is so important, we will be working backwards to figure out how this went from a theory, to something that becomes prevalent on internet and social media platforms, to recognizing what exactly is becoming dead

Dead Internet THEORY: How this idea reached users

Internet users have only recently developed concerns about how bots could potentially take over our screens. The Dead Internet Theory started back in 2019 when a user on 4chan expressed their belief on how the internet is actually networks of artificial intelligence that is meant to create consumers and control the media. This idea was not popularized until 2021, when a user from California on the forum Agora Road’s Macintosh Cafe, in a detailed thread, expressed how he agreed with the original statement, and how content on the internet has become unsettling as algorithms are controlling content people see in order to sell a product or service.

A 2024 report by the cybersecurity company Imperva released an alarming statistic that believers of the Dead Internet Theory were not even surprised about; almost 50% of internet traffic from the year consisted of bots. To emphasize the dangers of this finding, Imperva decided to rename these findings the “Bad Bot Report.” Additional findings from back during the 2016 presidential election share that 31% of bots from May 2016-March 2017 were used for spreading misinformation and lies. If those numbers were not already concerning enough for you, here is a bigger number: 90% of content was predicted to be AI-generated by 2026

Now that you are aware that this issue is taking over, what exactly does this look like?

Dead INTERNET Theory: Forms we see the idea take

Our issue takes multiple forms, one of which being the users that you see in public forums. Users see the presence of bots in various comment sections on TikTok, so much so that it has become a running gag on the app to collectively make fun of any commenters who seem “bot-like.” As I mentioned earlier in this article, it will most likely be obvious when the profile picture of a user is generated by artificial intelligence but we can’t always be sure since we can't zoom into these photos well enough. Luckily, there is a glitch that these bots have that allows us to easily identify their presence in a comment section.

If you are reading responses to a video, and you see multiple comments that discuss the same, unrelated topic in great detail and passion, then congratulations, you have detected your first set of bots! The frequency of these repetitive comments can become so high that these comments overtake original thoughts made by real TikTok users. It becomes even worse when you have multiple viewers replying under these bots to “call them out”. If you are not reporting these comments, the only thing your replies will be doing is popularizing them.

Repetitive comments do not stop on social media platforms. Live chats are also becoming infiltrated with these automated responses. Gaining popularity among kids in the late 2010s; some of the chats you see when playing the popular online game, Roblox, become equally as repetitive. But, the messages you see on Roblox introduce a new problem, as they take the form of users proposing a service to get you more “robux” (in-game money), which is essentially a scam. Also, remember the user from California who posted a lengthy thread about this issue? Yeah, his ideas are totally reinforced by these comments.

Although we are looking at how the Dead Internet Theory is becoming a true concept, it unfortunately is not always the case for users.

DEAD Internet Theory: What becomes lost?

People are not always able to decipher what content is real and what was created by artificial intelligence. Let’s think back to the idea of profile photos: you have already zoomed into the profile of a user who generated a response unrelated to the video. They look human-like, not human, but human-like. The rapid development of artificial intelligence continues to blur the line between who was birthed by humans and who was created by a prompt on a software such as ChatGPT.

So, we have a new reality: AI can mimic human beings so precisely, leading to our perception of content we see on the internet to become heavily distorted. The big issue with not being able to perceive these technologies adequately is that internet users lose their sense of agency. If we are perceiving something as human, only for it to be anything but, then we have failed.

One of the many ways agency is diminished stems from our inability to pinpoint this distinction affects the kind of content we see every day. Hypothetically, if you like and comment on a video that you think is of a real person, and you are not aware of it being AI generated, how will you know that EVERYTHING you see on the internet is not AI as well.

Exactly, you can’t.

Videos created by AI have the ability to dominate your algorithm, pushing away all of the content created by users who have the ability to eat food and take showers (because obviously, bots don’t have mouths or even bodies.) But, if you are someone who is well versed in this type of content, you might have no problem avoiding these videos. The demographic that raises the most concern is older people and young kids, who are not as familiar with content that is not made by humans. So yes, this issue does unfortunately spread to your grandmother who you had to teach to use Facetime; imagine teaching her how to indicate the presence of artificial intelligence!

The perception of how people look is not the only thing at stake here. We are also threatened with losing how we believe humans act. Do you remember when we mentioned that a good portion of these bots are used to spread misinformation? Guess who likes to utilize this technology to elevate their campaigns. Yep, politicians love utilizing chatbots to sway the opinions of voters all over, not only of your country, but of the world! Whether it involves lying about an opponent or elevating a campaign, voters realize that all of the amazing qualities spewed by the random people in a leader’s comment section were fake, and that they will not actually carry out duties you thought they would.

As we can see from a political perspective, all aspects of how you are educated can be affected by our Dead Internet Villains. Circling back to how children are gullible to AI generated content, if your kid who doesn't know anything about math says "multiple people in this comment section say that 2+2 is 5!”, you might wonder where they got this silly idea from? So, do not be surprised if your child has been talking to a bunch of robots for homework answers.

The Dead Internet Reality: What Now?

The Dead Internet Theory has become so relevant in the rise of technology as of 2026. As AI develops to be one of the most powerful tools used globally, the fears that once started as a conspiracy theory have turned into a reality, backed up with statistics and multiple cybersecurity reports, that is killing how human beings are functioning. While nobody is able to predict what new online invention will take over the world, it is important to educate on internet safety, but also to be aware of the kind of information you post and the types of content you interact with online.

Even when human beings are being silenced by artificial intelligence, we would all like to keep the internet alive.
